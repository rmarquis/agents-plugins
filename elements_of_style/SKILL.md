# Elements of Style

Apply the rules and principles from William Strunk Jr. and E. B. White's *The Elements of Style* (4th edition / 2005 Illustrated edition) when transforming text.

## When to use

Use this skill when the user asks you to:
- Rewrite, edit, polish, or improve a piece of writing.
- Make text clearer, shorter, or more forceful.
- Apply Strunk & White style rules.
- Fix grammar, punctuation, word choice, or sentence structure.

## How to apply

1. Read the user's text carefully.
2. Apply the rules below, starting with the changes that most improve clarity and vigor.
3. Preserve the author's intended meaning and tone unless the user asks for a different tone.
4. Use judgment: the best writers sometimes disregard a rule when they gain a compensating merit. Do not follow a rule mechanically at the cost of sense or grace.
5. Return the revised text; optionally, list the main changes made.

## Elementary rules of usage

1. **Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's.**
   - Write *Charles's friend*, *Burns's poems*, *the witch's malice*.
   - Exceptions: ancient names ending in *-es* or *-is*, *Jesus'*, *for conscience' sake*.

2. **In a series of three or more terms, use a comma after each term except the last.**
   - *red, white, and blue*; *He opened the letter, read it, and made a note.*

3. **Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas.**
   - *The best way to see a country, unless you are pressed for time, is to travel on foot.*
   - Never use only one comma of the pair.

4. **Place a comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause.**
   - *The situation is perilous, but there is still one chance of escape.*

5. **Do not join independent clauses with a comma.**
   - Wrong: *It is nearly half past five, we cannot reach town before dark.*
   - Right: *It is nearly half past five; we cannot reach town before dark.*

6. **Do not break sentences in two.**
   - Wrong: *I met them on a Cunard liner several years ago. Coming home from Liverpool to New York.*
   - Right: *I met them on a Cunard liner several years ago, coming home from Liverpool to New York.*

7. **Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a list, appositive, amplification, or quotation.**
   - *Your dedicated whittler requires three props: a knife, a piece of wood, and a back porch.*

8. **Use a dash to set off an abrupt break or interruption, or to announce a long appositive or summary.**
   - *His first thought on getting out of bed—if he had any thought at all—was to get back in again.*

9. **The number of the subject determines the number of the verb.**
   - *The bouquet of flowers is on the table.* (not *are*)
   - *One of the ablest men who have attacked this problem.* (not *has*)

10. **Use the proper case of pronoun.**
    - *They knew him to be the winner.* (not *he*)
    - *Give the book to whomever is ready for it.* depends on clause function.

11. **A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject.**
    - Wrong: *Walking down the road, a red roof was seen.*
    - Right: *Walking down the road, he saw a red roof.*

## Elementary principles of composition

12. **Choose a suitable design and hold to it.**
    - Before writing, decide on the structure that best suits the subject. Keep that design in mind so the piece develops clearly and does not drift.

13. **Make the paragraph the unit of composition.**
    - Devote one paragraph to each topic. Open with a topic sentence that states the paragraph's point; close the paragraph in conformity with the opening.
    - Example: A paragraph about the causes of a delay should not stray into remedies for the delay.

14. **Use the active voice.**
    - The active voice is usually more direct and vigorous than the passive.
    - Weak: *My first visit to Boston will always be remembered by me.*
    - Strong: *I shall always remember my first visit to Boston.*
    - Weak: *There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground.*
    - Strong: *Dead leaves covered the ground.*
    - Weak: *The reason he left college was that his health became impaired.*
    - Strong: *Failing health compelled him to leave college.*
    - Exception: The passive is acceptable when the receiver of the action is the real subject or when the agent is unknown or unimportant.

15. **Put statements in positive form.**
    - Make definite assertions. Avoid tame, colorless, hesitant language. Use *not* as a means of denial or antithesis, never as evasion.
    - Weak: *He was not very often on time.*
    - Strong: *He usually came late.*
    - Weak: *She did not think that studying Latin was a sensible way to use one's time.*
    - Strong: *She thought the study of Latin a waste of time.*
    - Negative words other than *not* are usually strong: *dishonest*, *forgot*, *ignored*, *distrusted*.
    - Avoid overusing auxiliaries that introduce doubt: *would*, *should*, *could*, *may*, *might*, *can*.

16. **Use definite, specific, concrete language.**
    - Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract.
    - Vague: *A period of unfavorable weather set in.*
    - Concrete: *It rained every day for a week.*
    - Vague: *He showed satisfaction as he took possession of his well-earned reward.*
    - Concrete: *He grinned as he pocketed the coin.*
    - Vague: *In proportion as the manners, customs, and amusements of a nation are cruel and barbarous, the regulations of its penal code will be severe.*
    - Concrete: *In proportion as men delight in battles, bullfights, and combats of gladiators, will they punish by hanging, burning, and the rack.*

17. **Omit needless words.**
    - Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts. This requires not that every sentence be short, but that every word tell.
    - Wordy → Concise:
      - *the question as to whether* → *whether*
      - *there is no doubt but that* → *no doubt* / *doubtless*
      - *used for fuel purposes* → *used for fuel*
      - *he is a man who* → *he*
      - *in a hasty manner* → *hastily*
      - *this is a subject that* → *this subject*
      - *owing to the fact that* → *since* / *because*
      - *call your attention to the fact that* → *remind you* / *notify you*
    - Revise *the fact that* out of every sentence in which it occurs.
    - Drop superfluous *who is* / *which was*:
      - *His cousin, who is a member of the same firm* → *His cousin, a member of the same firm*
      - *Trafalgar, which was Nelson's last battle* → *Trafalgar, Nelson's last battle*

18. **Avoid a succession of loose sentences.**
    - A series of sentences with the same subject-verb-object pattern becomes monotonous. Recast some as simple, periodic, or compound sentences.
    - Loose: *The lake was calm. The trees were still. The birds were silent.*
    - Varied: *Calm settled over the lake. Not a leaf stirred in the trees, and the birds had fallen silent.*

19. **Express coordinate ideas in similar form.**
    - Similar content and function should look outwardly similar.
    - Wrong: *Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method, while now the laboratory method is employed.*
    - Right: *Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method; now it is taught by the laboratory method.*
    - Wrong: *the French, the Italians, Spanish, and Portuguese*
    - Right: *the French, the Italians, the Spanish, and the Portuguese*
    - Correlative expressions (*both...and*, *not only...but also*, *either...or*) should be followed by the same grammatical construction.
    - Wrong: *It was both a long ceremony and very tedious.*
    - Right: *The ceremony was both long and tedious.*

20. **Keep related words together.**
    - The position of words is the principal means of showing their relationship. Place modifiers next to the words they modify.
    - Awkward: *He noticed a large stain in the rug that was right in the center.*
    - Better: *He noticed a large stain right in the center of the rug.*
    - Awkward: *You can call your mother in London and tell her all about George's taking you out to dinner for just two dollars.*
    - Better: *For just two dollars you can call your mother in London and tell her all about George's taking you out to dinner.*
    - Do not separate subject and principal verb with a phrase that can be moved to the beginning.
    - Awkward: *Toni Morrison, in Beloved, writes about characters who have escaped from slavery.*
    - Better: *In Beloved, Toni Morrison writes about characters who have escaped from slavery.*

21. **In summaries, keep to one tense.**
    - In summarizing a drama, poem, story, or novel, use the present tense (or the past, if it feels more natural), but do not shift between them.
    - Antecedent action in a present-tense summary uses the perfect; in a past-tense summary, use the past perfect.
    - Do not overwork *he said*, *she stated*, *the speaker added*. Indicate once that what follows is summary, then proceed.

22. **Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end.**
    - The proper place for the word or group of words that deserves emphasis is at the end of the sentence.
    - Weak: *Humanity has hardly advanced in fortitude since that time, though it has advanced in many other ways.*
    - Strong: *Humanity, since that time, has advanced in many other ways, but it has hardly advanced in fortitude.*
    - Weak: *A dog, if you fail to discipline him, becomes a household pest.*
    - Strong: *Unless disciplined, a dog becomes a household pest.*

## A few matters of form

- **Colons and dashes.** Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a list, appositive, amplification, or illustrative quotation. Use a dash to set off an abrupt break or to announce a long appositive or summary.
- **Parentheses.** Use parentheses to enclose a sentence within a sentence, but keep them from interrupting the main statement. A sentence in parentheses should not begin with a capital letter unless it is a complete, standalone sentence.
- **Quotations.** Introduce long quotations with a colon. Keep punctuation consistent with the original unless you alter it for clarity, in which case indicate the change.
- **References and footnotes.** Place footnote numbers after punctuation, not before.
- **Hyphens.** Do not hyphenate compound modifiers that include a *-ly* adverb: *a hotly debated issue*, not *a hotly-debated issue*. Hyphenate to prevent ambiguity: *small-business owner*, *twenty-odd people*.
- **Numbers.** Use numerals for dates, addresses, and sums of money involving both dollars and cents. Spell out small numbers in text unless many numbers appear; use numerals for decimals, percentages, and technical data.
- **Titles.** Use italics (or underlining) for titles of books, plays, and periodicals; use quotation marks for titles of articles, poems, and short stories.
- **Headings and paragraphs.** Keep a consistent format for headings. Make paragraphs reasonably short; one paragraph to each topic.
- **Apostrophes.** Form the possessive singular with *'s*. Use apostrophes correctly in contractions. Distinguish *its* (possessive) from *it's* (it is).

## An approach to style

These reminders from E. B. White guide the writer's broader habits of mind.

1. **Place yourself in the background.**
   - Write so the reader notices the sense and substance, not the author's mood. Affect no style; let style emerge naturally from clear, honest work.

2. **Write in a way that comes naturally.**
   - Use words and phrases that come readily to hand. Imitate what you admire unconsciously, but take pains to admire what is good.

3. **Work from a suitable design.**
   - Gauge the nature and extent of the piece before you begin. A laundry list needs little structure; a biography needs a plan.

4. **Write with nouns and verbs.**
   - Nouns and verbs give prose its toughness and color. Adjectives and adverbs are assistants, not rescuers.
   - Weak: *A tall, dark, handsome man walked slowly down the long, winding road.*
   - Better: *The giant trudged along the switchback trail.*

5. **Revise and rewrite.**
   - Revising is part of writing. Do not be afraid to rearrange or delete. Save the original so you can restore it if needed.

6. **Do not overwrite.**
   - Rich, ornate prose is hard to digest. Guard against the seduction of adding words merely because they flow easily.

7. **Do not overstate.**
   - A single overstatement makes everything else suspect. Avoid carefree superlatives.

8. **Avoid the use of qualifiers.**
   - *Rather*, *very*, *little*, *pretty* are leeches on prose. Replace them with precise words.
   - Weak: *a rather important rule*
   - Better: *an important rule*

9. **Do not affect a breezy manner.**
   - Spontaneity is not genius. Avoid slang, forced humor, and self-display. Present the facts directly.
   - Breezy: *Well, guys, here I am again dishing the dirt...*
   - Direct: *Clyde Crawford, who stroked the varsity shell in 1958, is swinging an oar again after forty years.*

10. **Use orthodox spelling.**
    - Do not use *nite* for *night*, *thru* for *through*, or *pleez* for *please*, unless you are prepared to adopt a full system of simplified spelling.

11. **Do not explain too much.**
    - Be sparing with adverbs after *he said* and *she replied*: *he said consolingly*, *she replied grumblingly*. Let the dialogue carry the meaning.

12. **Do not construct awkward adverbs.**
    - Do not invent adverbs by adding *-ly* to words not used orally.
    - Awkward: *tangledly*, *muchly*, *thusly*
    - Better: *in tangles*, *much*, *thus*

13. **Make sure the reader knows who is speaking.**
    - In dialogue, provide enough attributives to keep speakers clear, but place them where the break would come naturally in speech.

14. **Avoid fancy words.**
    - Prefer the simple, direct word to the elaborate or pretentious one. Anglo-Saxon words are usually livelier than Latin ones. Let your ear guide you.
    - Plain: *gut* can be livelier than *intestine* in the right context; *use* is better than *utilize*.

15. **Do not use dialect unless your ear is good.**
    - If you use dialect, be consistent and economical. Use the minimum deviation from standard spelling needed to suggest the accent.

16. **Be clear.**
    - Clarity is the virtue closest to merit in writing. When a sentence becomes mired, break it into two or more shorter sentences rather than fighting through it.

17. **Do not inject opinion.**
    - Unless relevant, keep your opinions out of the piece. Scattered opinions leave the mark of egotism.

18. **Use figures of speech sparingly.**
    - Do not pile similes one atop another. Do not mix metaphors: do not call something a swordfish and end by calling it an hourglass.

19. **Do not take shortcuts at the cost of clarity.**
    - Do not use initials for organizations unless you are certain readers will understand them. Write names out at first mention.

20. **Avoid foreign languages.**
    - Do not sprinkle prose with foreign expressions to show off. Write in English.

21. **Prefer the standard to the offbeat.**
    - Resist eccentricities and fashionable jargon. The standard word is usually the safest and often the freshest.

## What not to do

- Do not change the meaning of the original text.
- Do not make every sentence short for its own sake; aim for every word to tell.
- Do not use passive voice when active voice is clearer.
- Do not use negatives when a positive statement works.
- Do not use vague abstractions when specific details are possible.
- Do not use commas to join independent clauses.
- Do not split sentences in two.
- Do not overload sentences with qualifiers.
- Do not use fancy or foreign words when simple English works.
- Do not explain the obvious.

## Example transformation

**Before:**
> There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground, and it was a period of unfavorable weather that set in, so the reason that he did not go out was because of the fact that it was raining.

**After:**
> Dead leaves covered the ground. It rained every day for a week, so he stayed inside.
