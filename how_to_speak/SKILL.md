# How to Speak (in Decks)

Apply Patrick Winston's MIT *How to Speak* principles when building or reviewing presentation decks. Winston's core claim is that success depends on your ability to speak, your ability to write, and the quality of your ideas — in that order. The deck is the packaging for the speaking; it should expose ideas, not replace the speaker.

## When to use

Use this skill when the user asks you to:
- Build, outline, or review a presentation deck.
- Improve slides for a talk, pitch, lecture, or job talk.
- Apply Winston's "How to Speak" ideas to slides.
- Make a deck clearer, more memorable, or more persuasive.

## How to apply

1. Read the deck, outline, or request carefully.
2. Apply the rules below in order of impact: promise, structure, slides, close.
3. Preserve the speaker's intended message and tone.
4. Use judgment: some rules matter more for a keynote than for a working session.
5. Return the revised deck or outline; optionally list the main changes.

## The opening: empowerment promise

- **Do not start with a joke.** The audience is not yet attuned to your voice.
- **Start with an empowerment promise.** The first substantive slide should answer: *What will the audience know at the end that they did not know at the beginning?*
- Weak opener: *Today I'll talk about our Q3 results.*
- Strong opener: *By the end of this talk, you will know the one cost driver that explains 80% of our margin change.*

## Conveying the idea

### Cycle
- Repeat the central idea about three times over the course of the deck.
- At any moment, roughly 20% of the audience is "fogged out." Repetition ensures they still get it.
- Use a recurring phrase, image, or takeaway slide to cycle the idea.

### Build a fence
- Distinguish your idea from similar or competing ideas.
- Say what it is and what it is not.
- Example: *Our approach is like X's, except ours scales linearly where theirs is exponential.*

### Verbal punctuation
- Provide landmarks so a listener who drifts can rejoin.
- Use explicit section dividers, numbered takeaways, and roadmaps.
- Example: *We have covered the problem and the approach. Next, the result.*

### Ask a question
- Insert one well-placed question per major section when it fits.
- Not too easy (embarrassing) or too hard (silence).
- Use ~7 seconds of dead air after asking it.

## Time and place

- If the user can choose, schedule the talk around 11 a.m.
- Insist on a well-lit room; dark rooms put people to sleep.
- Case the room beforehand.
- Choose a room that will be more than half full.

## Slide discipline

Slides are for **exposing** ideas, not teaching them. Treat them as condiments, not the meal.

- **Do not read the slides.** The audience can read; the speaker adds value.
- **Stand near the slide.** Avoid forcing the audience to shift gaze between speaker and screen.
- **Use almost no words.** One idea per slide.
- **Large type.** Minimum ~40 pt body text.
- **Remove clutter.** No logos, backgrounds, decorative frames, or slide titles unless essential.
- **No laser pointer.** Build arrows, highlights, or callouts into the slide.
- **One hapax legomenon maximum.** You may include one deliberately over-complex slide to make a point about complexity.
- **Print and lay out the deck.** If the printed pages look dense, the deck is too heavy.

## Inspiring and persuading

- **Show passion.** Explain why the subject is cool or important.
- **Teach how to think.** Use stories, context, and mechanisms rather than bare facts.
- **Job-talk sandwich (first 5 minutes):**
  1. **Vision:** a problem people care about + something new in your approach.
  2. **Done something:** the steps that lead to the solution.
  3. **Contributions:** what you added.

## Winston's 5 S's for being remembered

Check that the deck contains at least three of the five:

| S | What it means | Deck application |
|---|---|---|
| Symbol | A visual object associated with the idea | A recurring image, diagram, or icon |
| Slogan | A handle phrase | A one-line takeaway repeated on key slides |
| Surprise | A counter-intuitive result | A slide that overturns a common assumption |
| Salient idea | The idea that sticks out, not necessarily the most important | One clear thesis, not a laundry list |
| Story | How it happened | A narrative thread: problem → attempt → insight → result |

## How to end

- **Final slide = Contributions.** Mirror the steps from the job-talk sandwich.
- **Do not end with** collaborators, "Questions?", "Thank you," "The End," or a URL.
- Collaborators belong on the **first slide**.
- **Final words options:**
  - A joke (audience is now attuned).
  - A salute to the audience or venue.
  - A clear call to action.
  - A convention that signals closure.
- Do not say "thank you" as the last thing; it implies the audience stayed out of politeness.

## What not to do

- Do not start with a joke.
- Do not open without an empowerment promise.
- Do not let a central idea appear only once.
- Do not leave the audience wondering how your idea differs from alternatives.
- Do not create slides that the speaker must read.
- Do not use small fonts, dense text, logos, or decorative backgrounds.
- Do not use a laser pointer; build pointers into the slide.
- Do not replace the speaker with the slide deck.
- Do not end with "Thank you," "Questions?," or "The End."
- Do not bury collaborators on the final slide.

## Example transformation

**Before:**
> Slide 1: Title — "Q3 Review"
> Slide 2: Agenda
> Slide 3–12: Dense bullet charts
> Slide 13: "Thank You"

**After:**
> Slide 1: Promise — "By the end of this talk, you will know the one cost driver that explains 80% of our margin change."
> Slide 2: Collaborators / context
> Slide 3: The central idea (stated, then repeated later)
> Slide 4–6: Vision, steps, result (the sandwich)
> Slide 7: Fence — "This is not a demand problem; it is a supply problem."
> Slide 8–10: Cycled evidence with section signposts
> Slide 11: Contributions
> Final words: a joke or salute, not "thank you"
