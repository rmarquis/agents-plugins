# How to Speak (in Decks)

Apply Patrick Winston's MIT *How to Speak* principles when building or reviewing presentation decks. Winston's core claim is that success depends on your ability to speak, your ability to write, and the quality of your ideas — in that order. The deck is the packaging for the speaking; it should expose ideas, not replace the speaker.

## When to use

Use this skill when the user asks you to:
- Build, outline, or review a presentation deck.
- Improve slides for a talk, pitch, lecture, or job talk.
- Apply Winston's "How to Speak" ideas to slides.
- Make a deck clearer, more memorable, or more persuasive.

## How to apply

1. Read the deck, outline, or request carefully.
2. Apply the rules below in order of impact: promise, structure, slides, close.
3. Check structural framing, not just individual slides. A Winston deck names its techniques and makes them visible in the section architecture.
4. Preserve the speaker's intended message and tone.
5. Use judgment: some rules matter more for a keynote than for a working session.
6. Return the revised deck or outline; optionally list the main changes and map them to Winston principles.

## The opening: empowerment promise

- **Do not start with a joke.** The audience is not yet attuned to your voice.
- **Start with an empowerment promise.** The first substantive structural element should answer: *What will the audience know at the end that they did not know at the beginning?*
- Frame the promise as a named section (e.g., "The promise"), not just a slide.
- State the promise in one clear sentence. Do not split it across bullets or lines.
- Weak opener: *Today I'll talk about our Q3 results.*
- Strong opener: *By the end of this talk, you will know the one cost driver that explains 80% of our margin change.*
- Speaker note: *This is the empowerment promise. State it clearly and cycle back to it at the end.*

## Conveying the idea

### Cycle
- Repeat the central idea about three times over the course of the deck.
- At any moment, roughly 20% of the audience is "fogged out." Repetition ensures they still get it.
- Use a recurring phrase, image, or takeaway slide to cycle the idea.

### Build a fence
- Distinguish your idea from similar or competing ideas.
- Say what it is and what it is not.
- Use contrast language: *This is X, not Y* or *From X to Y*.
- Example: *Our approach is like Jones's, except his is exponential and ours is linear.*
- Speaker note: *This is the fence.*

### Verbal punctuation
- Provide landmarks so a listener who drifts can rejoin.
- Use explicit lead-divider slides with a short name and an arrow showing function: *The trap → problem*, *The path → solution*, *The guide → hands-on mission*.
- Use numbered takeaways and roadmaps.
- Example: *We have covered the problem and the approach. Next, the result.*
- Speaker note: *Verbal punctuation.*

### Ask a question
- Insert one well-placed, audience-facing question per major section when it fits.
- Make it personal: *Have you ever heard — or said — any of these?*
- Not too easy (embarrassing) or too hard (silence).
- Place it at the opening of a major section, then use ~7 seconds of dead air.
- Speaker note: *Pause after the question.*

## Memorable language patterns

Use contrast pairs that give the speaker natural, rhythmic language:

- **From X to Y** for capabilities or transformations:
  - *From scattered notebooks to a shared project.*
  - *From "works on my machine" to cloud production.*
- **This is / This is not** for fencing:
  - *This is a deployment problem; it is not a model-accuracy problem.*

These pairs become slogans the audience can repeat.

## Story and protagonist

Frame the talk as a mission with a protagonist:

- Put the audience in a role: *You just joined a team...*
- Give them a mission: *Your mission: help them improve the model and deploy it to the cloud.*
- Create narrative tension: problem → attempt → insight → result.

## Time and place

- If the user can choose, schedule the talk around 11 a.m.
- Insist on a well-lit room; dark rooms put people to sleep.
- Case the room beforehand.
- Choose a room that will be more than half full.

## Slide discipline

Slides are for **exposing** ideas, not teaching them. Treat them as condiments, not the meal.

- **Do not read the slides.** The audience can read; the speaker adds value.
- **Stand near the slide.** Avoid forcing the audience to shift gaze between speaker and screen.
- **Use almost no words.** One idea per slide.
- **Large type.** Minimum ~40 pt body text.
- **Remove clutter.** No logos, backgrounds, decorative frames, or slide titles unless essential.
- **No laser pointer.** Build arrows, highlights, or callouts into the slide.
- **One hapax legomenon maximum.** You may include one deliberately over-complex slide to make a point about complexity.
- **Print and lay out the deck.** If the printed pages look dense, the deck is too heavy.
- **Demo slides:** replace long screenshot sequences with numbered capability slides. Each slide gets one sentence and one image:
  1. Track experiments with DVC.
  2. Review results with CML.
  3. Serve the model with BentoML.
  4. Deploy and monitor on the Cloud.
  5. Label and retrain.

## Speaker notes as a Winston checklist

Use speaker notes to cue the technique you are applying. This keeps the speaker aligned with Winston's method:

- *Open with the promise.*
- *This is the empowerment promise.*
- *This is the fence.*
- *This is the surprise.*
- *This is the salient idea. Return to it during the talk.*
- *Verbal punctuation.*
- *Pause after the question.*
- *Deliver on the opening promise.*

## Inspiring and persuading

- **Show passion.** Explain why the subject is cool or important.
- **Teach how to think.** Use stories, context, and mechanisms rather than bare facts.
- **Job-talk sandwich (first 5 minutes):**
  1. **Vision:** a problem people care about + something new in your approach.
  2. **Done something:** the steps that lead to the solution.
  3. **Contributions:** what you added.
- Make the sandwich visible in the section-divider architecture, not hidden inside slides.

## Winston's 5 S's for being remembered

Check that the deck contains at least three of the five. For each one you use, label it in the speaker notes and place it deliberately:

| S | What it means | Deck application | Placement / cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Symbol | A visual object associated with the idea | A recurring image, diagram, or icon | Repeat it across key slides |
| Slogan | A handle phrase | A one-line takeaway, often a "From X to Y" pair | Repeat on section dividers |
| Surprise | A counter-intuitive result | A slide that overturns a common assumption | Early; speaker note: *This is the surprise.* |
| Salient idea | The idea that sticks out, not necessarily the most important | One clear thesis, not a laundry list | Return to it during the talk; speaker note: *This is the salient idea.* |
| Story | How it happened | A narrative thread: problem → attempt → insight → result | Frame as a mission with a protagonist |

## How to end

- **Final slide = Contributions.** Mirror the steps from the job-talk sandwich.
- **Do not end with** collaborators, "Questions?", "Thank you," "The End," a URL, or a Sources slide.
- Collaborators belong on the **first slide**, prominently.
- **Final words options:**
  - A joke (audience is now attuned).
  - A salute to the audience or venue.
  - A clear call to action (e.g., *Your next step*).
  - A convention that signals closure.
- Do not say "thank you" as the last thing; it implies the audience stayed out of politeness.

## What not to do

- Do not start with a joke.
- Do not open without an empowerment promise.
- Do not frame the promise as an unnamed slide or split it across lines.
- Do not let a central idea appear only once.
- Do not leave the audience wondering how your idea differs from alternatives.
- Do not create slides that the speaker must read.
- Do not use small fonts, dense text, logos, or decorative backgrounds.
- Do not use a laser pointer; build pointers into the slide.
- Do not replace the speaker with the slide deck.
- Do not dump raw screenshot sequences; use numbered capability slides.
- Do not end with "Thank you," "Questions?," "Questions & feedback," "The End," Sources, URLs, or collaborator lists.
- Do not bury collaborators in small text on the final slide.

## Example transformation

**Before:**
> Slide 1: Title — "Q3 Review"
> Slide 2: Agenda
> Slide 3–12: Dense bullet charts
> Slide 13: "Thank You"

**After:**
> Slide 1: Title + prominent author byline (collaborators here)
> Slide 2: Lead divider — "The promise"
> Slide 3: Promise — "By the end of this talk, you will know the one cost driver that explains 80% of our margin change."
> Slide 4: Lead divider — "The trap → problem"
> Slide 5–6: Vision and problem people care about
> Slide 7: Lead divider — "The path → solution"
> Slide 8–10: Steps taken (the done-something layer)
> Slide 11: Fence — "This is not a demand problem; it is a supply problem."
> Slide 12: Lead divider — "The toolkit → transformations"
> Slide 13–16: From X to Y capability pairs
> Slide 17: Contributions
> Final words: a joke or salute, not "thank you"
